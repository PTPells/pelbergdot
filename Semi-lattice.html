Semi-lattices and side projects

A couple of years ago, I designed a free-standing kitchen island/countertop for our apartment. We had the island made from a ~3" thick, 75" long piece of recycled eucalyptus butcher block.
The block needed to be cut down some to meet our needs and the offcuts ended up being large enough to make four cutting boards.

I imagined the process for making the first cutting board would be straightforward considering the planks had already been glued-up and planed. I would need to fill in the imperfections, sand the block to 220 grit, chamfer the edges, moisturize it with mineral oil, and seal it with beeswax. These were all operations I had experience with or could clearly visualize, except for the “fill in the imperfections” part.

In any case, I did the filling-in, sanding, chamfering, moisturizing, and sealing. I did not encounter any issues in the process. Then, when I went to rinse the board off after using it for the first time, I noticed an off-white substance running off the board into the sink. I pieced together that this was the wood glue + sawdust combination I'd used to fill in the board’s imperfections. I came to understand wood glue is water soluble; the cutting board was not usable in its current form.

This information was new and inconvenient. I lost enthusiasm for the project soon after.

Meanwhile, we moved into a new apartment and needed more counter space in the kitchen for our coffee set up. The Ikea Bror cart fit the space well and I planned to replace its plywood top with something that would soften the cart’s industrial powder-coated steel frame and compliment its heft. I found a slab of sycamore I thought would work well and I proceeded to finish it.

Before sanding the sycamore slab, I needed to fill in a few places where the wood had partially split. The same problem as before, but this time, I used epoxy. The epoxy worked well and I became comfortable working with it.

While working on the coffee cart, Sara wanted to go to Urban Ore. There, I found a solid, albeit rusty, Stanley Chisel. I figured some steel wool, rust remover, and a whetstone could bring it back into a usable state. This worked, and in the process, it occurred to me that I could use the now-functional chisel to excavate the hardened wood glue and sawdust from the cutting board that had been sitting for 6+ months. I also had epoxy left over from the sycamore slab. These new tools – the epoxy and the chisel – and the know-how to use them, summed into the motivation and resources I needed to finish the cutting board, which I ended up doing.

We’ve been using the cutting board and coffee cart without issue for a few months now. Within this usage, and a reflection on the process of making these two pieces, is some information I would like to keep in mind when working on side projects going forward: projects relate in many ways and these relationships are difficult to see before starting.

After learning the wood glue-sawdust mixture was not sufficient, I left the cutting board out in our living room as a reminder for me to continue working on it.

After a couple of weekends passed without doing any additional work on the cutting board, I started to feel weighed down by its “unfinishedness.” I resisted starting work on other projects, not wanting to move onto a new one without having finished the previous one.

Eventually, I got tired of looking at the cutting boards and I moved them to the closet. Sometime after, I started work on the coffee cart project and, well, we already went through this part of the story.

Thinking back on all of this, I grew curious why I was unwilling to afford myself the space to subvert the sequence I had planned.
